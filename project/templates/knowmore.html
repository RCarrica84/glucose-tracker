How our app does the calculations?

If you are using background and meal-time insulin therapy (longacting insulin + rapid-acting insulin or on an insulin pump), you may benefit from using an insulin-to-carbohydrate ratio (ICR) and a insulin sensitivity factor (ISF) to determine your meal-time insulin dose. Learning to adjust your insulin dose to the amount of food you eat provides flexibility with eating. It requires a good understanding of your medicines and carbohydrate or “carb” counting.
The following text is for understanding how our app does the calculations and does NOT constitute medical advice.

Understanding ICR

What Is An Insulin-to-Carb Ratio (ICR)? An insulin-to-carb ratio helps you dose how much rapid-acting insulin you need to “cover” the carbohydrate you will eat at a meal or snack. For example, some people might take 1.5 units for every carb choice, or others might take 1 unit for every 10 grams of carb. Your health care provider or registered dietitian — who may also be a certified diabetes educator (CDE) — can help you choose a starting ratio; however, it may take experimentation before you find the correct insulin-to-carb ratio for you. Records of what you ate, the estimated amount of carbohydrate in your meal, how much insulin you took, and what your blood glucose was before and two hours after you ate will help you decide if the ratio is correct, or if it should be adjusted. Different people have different insulin-to-carb ratios. Additionally, insulin-to-carb ratios may change over the course of your lifetime or even throughout the day. Some people have one ratio for breakfast and a different ratio for lunch and dinner.

Undertanding ISF

What Is An Insulin Sensitivity Factor (ISF)? The insulin sensitivity factor (sometimes called an insulin correction factor) is used to calculate the amount of insulin you need to bring your blood glucose into target range. This adjusts or corrects a blood glucose level that may be higher or lower than desired before a meal. The correction dose is added to, or subtracted from, the pre-meal insulin dose. For example, some people might take 1 unit if blood glucose is 50 mg/dL higher than the target, and others might take 1 unit for every 25 mg/dL higher than the target. Your health care provider will help you determine your insulin correction factor as you begin working with this.

Target glucose

Target glucose (Correction target) is a value for calculating insulin, and is individually determined by healthcare providers to help manage high blood sugar. This personal goal is used in the correction formula to calculate the amount of insulin needed to bring elevated blood glucose back toward the target. While a target of 100 mg/dL is often used as a simple, default, or example target in educational materials to illustrate how a insulin sensitivity factor (ISF) works, it is too strict for many individuals.

Your health care provider should give you individualized guidelines for what your blood glucose range should be for safety and good health.

Pre-bolus target range

The pre-bolus target range is the ideal blood glucose level before taking rapid-acting insulin and eating. Is generally designed to get blood sugar as close to a non-diabetic range as possible, typically between 80–130 mg/dL (4.4–7.2 mmol/L) for adults. For children, the target is often slightly wider due to higher variability.

Pre-bolus Target Ranges: Adults (Type 1 or Type 2): 80–130 mg/dL (4.4–7.2 mmol/L). Children/Teens (13–19 years): 90–130 mg/dL (5.0–7.2 mmol/L). Children/Teens (13–19 years): 90–130 mg/dL (5.0–7.2 mmol/L). Children (6–12 years): 90–180 mg/dL (5.0–10.0 mmol/L). Children (less than 6 years): 100–180 mg/dL (5.5–10.0 mmol/L).

Safe lows/ Safe highs

Based on the American Diabetes Association (ADA) 2026 Standards of Care, safe blood sugar targets for most non-pregnant adults with diabetes are generally:
Low Target (Hypoglycemia boundary): Less than 70 mg/dL (3.9 mmol/L).
High Target (Hyperglycemia boundary): 180 mg/dL (10.0 mmol/L) or higher 1–2 hours after meals
Safe Lows (Hypoglycemia)
Low (Level 1): Blood glucose under 70 mg/dL (3.9 mmol/L). This is the point at which you should treat with 15 grams of fast-acting carbs.
Severe Low (Level 2): Blood glucose under 54 mg/dL (3.0 mmol/L). This indicates a high risk for dangerous symptoms (confusion, passing out) and requires immediate, urgent treatment, often requiring assistance from another person.
Safe Highs (Hyperglycemia)
Pre-meal Range: 80–130 mg/dL (4.4–7.2 mmol/L).
Post-meal Range: Less than 180 mg/dL (10.0 mmol/L).
Very High: 181–250 mg/dL is considered above range.
Warning Zone: If blood glucose is 250 mg/dL or higher, particularly if you are on insulin, you should check for ketones.

Putting it all together

Step 1: Calculate an insulin dose for food:

1. Add up the grams of carbohydrate in the foods you will eat.

2. Divide the total grams of carb by your insulin-to-carb ratio (ICR).

Total Grams Of Carbohydrate to be Eaten ÷ Insulin-to-Carb Ratio

Example Let’s say you plan to eat 45 grams of carbohydrate and your insulin-to-carb ratio is 1 unit of insulin for every 15 grams of carbohydrate eaten. To figure out how much insulin to give, divide 45 by 15.

45 Grams of Carbohydrate ÷ 15 = 3 units of insulin is needed for this amount of carbohydrate

Step 2: How to use your correction factor to reach your target blood glucose

1. Subtract your target blood glucose from your current blood glucose. 2. Divide the difference by your correction factor.

Step 3: Add the insulin needed for carbs to the insulin to correct high blood glucose for your total dose:

Example from steps 1 and 2: 3 units for food (carbs) + 2 units to correct high blood glucose = 5 units (total dose)
